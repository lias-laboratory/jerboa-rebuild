\section{Introduction}

Nowadays, geometric modelers are everywhere to be found, for example, in
archaeology, architecture and mechanical engineering, just to name a few
domains.
%
These tools allow to design and maintain precise and complexe works.
%
To do so, they rely on geometric modeling kernels (\textit{ACIS},
\textit{Parasolid}, \textit{OpenCascade}, etc) whose API provide them with the
necessary tools.
%
Thus, their importance has grown to the point to where they are essential.
One of the features of these kernels is the topological change (or
\emph{events}) tracking (creation, deletion, split, merging, constancy and
modification).
%
During the construction of a model, the applied operations can change both its
topology and geometry and the subsequent events need to be detected.
%
Since the tracking intervenes during the construction of a model to manage cells
identification and consistency, then it is an ad hoc solution to help maintain
the models consistency and, hence, is a key feature proposed by kernels.

\subsection*{Motivations}

Events detection has a variety of purpose such as the construction of bulletin
boards, persistent naming schemes and modeling parallelization.

A bulletin board  is a data structure describing the  cells evolution during the
construction of  a model  in a  form of  history. Hence,  their use  to maintain
models consistency.
%
Persistent naming schemes can depend on bulletin boards, and indirectly events
detection, to propose unique names for cells and, thus, allowing the
reevaluation of models or their transfer towards other systems.
%
Events detection could also show its usefulness in the parallelization of
modeling operations.

\subsection*{Dynamic and hybrid detection}
In its dynamic conception, an event detection method requires to compare a model
before and after the application of an operation.
%
As a result, the tracking may become a heavy task to perform and its time
cost may become significant.
%
Moreover, considering how models tend to grow in size during their conception,
the tracking may slow down over time and the more elaborated the model, the more
coherence issues may arise.

An hybrid conception of events detection would allow, depending on the
operations, to analyze only the modified part of the models. This approach, in
comparison to the dynamic one, is expected to reduce time by analyzing a model
subset. However, this approach requires the identify, from the coding of the
operation, the specific cells being modified which could be the model itself.

\subsection*{Our approach}

The proposed approach, here, is a method to statically detect events from the
modeling operations. To do so, our method is based on the usage of a graph
transformation rule formalism as operations. The analysis of such rules allow to
obtain events information without the need to apply them and, in most cases,
independent from the modified models. For certain operations, this method is
able to detect a potential split or merging. In theses cases, a dynamic analysis
of the models are needed to assert the event.
