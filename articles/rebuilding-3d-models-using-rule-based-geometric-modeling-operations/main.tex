	%% Abstract
	\begin{abstract}
		\begin{linenumbers}
			Designing complex 3D models can be a tedious process. %
			To ease this task, current parametric modeling systems offer a rebuild
			feature that allows users to modify the design process of a model and
			regenerate it. %
			However, such an automatic reconstruction raises the issue of
			characterizing topological entities, known as the persistent naming
			problem. %
			To address this challenge, most modeling systems require tracking the
			evolution of a large number of topological entities — which can quickly
			become costly — and often restrict editing to the modification of
			geometric parameters — thereby limiting reconstruction capabilities. %

			In this paper, we present a rebuild mechanism that tracks only the
			evolution of strictly necessary topological entities and allows users
			to add and delete operations as part of the editing process. %
			Our approach relies on two formalisms: representing objects using
			generalized maps, and defining modeling operations through graph
			transformation rules. %
			This ensures a homogeneous representation of topological entities and
			operations. %
			One of the advantages of using graph transformation rules is that
			operations can also be syntactically analyzed to automatically detect
			topological changes affecting topological entities — referred to as
			events — such as creation, splitting, merging, deletion, or modification. %
			As a result, our rebuild mechanism can automatically track the
			evolution of topological entities during both the construction and
			reconstruction processes. %
			Altogether, it enables reconstituting a topological entity's history,
			annotating relationships between entities and events across
			operations, and matching equivalent candidate entities when rebuilding. %

			Finally, our rebuild mechanism allows users to explicitly choose match
			candidates and keep control on the rebuild process.
		\end{linenumbers}
	\end{abstract}
