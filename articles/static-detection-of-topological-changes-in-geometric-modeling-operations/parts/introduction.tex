\section{Introduction}

Nowadays, geometric modelers are everywhere to be found, for example, in
archaeology, architecture and mechanical engineering, just to name a few
domains. These tools allow to design and maintain precise and complexe works. To
do so, they rely on geometric modeling kernels (\textit{ACIS},
\textit{Parasolid}, \textit{OpenCascade}, etc) whose API provide them with the
necessary tools. Thus, their importance has grown to the point to where they are
essential. One of the features of these kernels is the topological change (or
\emph{events}) tracking (creation, deletion, split, merging, constancy and
modification). During the construction of a model, the applied operations can
change both its topology and geometry and the subsequent events need to be
detected. Since the tracking intervenes during the construction of a model to
manage cells identification and consistency, then it is an ad hoc solution to
help maintain the models consistency and, hence, is a key feature proposed by
kernels.

\subsection*{Motivations}

Events detection has a variety of purpose such as the construction of bulletin
boards, persistent naming schemes and modeling optimisation A bulletin board is
a data structure describing the cells evolution during the construction of a
model in a form of history. Such an information is needed for the internal
execution of a number of operations provided by a kernel and is also passed
through the API in order to help developers using these kernels to maintain the
models consistency. \maxime{Revoir cette phrase} A typical use of these bulletin
boards is their integration within persistent naming schemes in parametric
systems. Persistent naming schemes depend directly or indirectly on events
detection to propose unique and persistent names for cells, thus, allowing the
reevaluation of models regardless of parameter change, or their transfer towards
other systems. Events detection is also useful in the optimisation of modeling
operations.

\subsection*{Static and dynamic detection}
An event detection can be performed either dynamically or statically

A static detection, performs the analysis of the operations only. Although they
can be tailored for specific geometry, operations are independent of the models
on which they are applied. And since operations are not expected to change
during modeling, then events can be computed before run-time and are known
before any application.

Dynamic detection, however, requires to compare a model before and after any
operation application. As a result the time efficiency of a dynamic method
directly depends on the number of cells existing in a model. Therefore, and
considering how models tend to become more complex during their construction,
the time cost to fully perform the events tracking may dramatically increase.
Moreover, since a dynamic event detection can be completed after the application
of a operation only, then possible consistency issues cannot be prevented.

A static detection is expected to be less time consuming compared to a dynamic
one. However, this approach requires the identify modified cells and resulting
events from the coding of the operation. \maxime{Remplacé par statique (et
  paragraphe ré-écrit)}%

\subsection*{Our approach}
The analysis of an operation, although based on a well-known algorithm, may
encounter some limitations since its coding is dependent of the developing team.

The proposed approach, here, relies on generalized maps to formalise the models
and graph transformation rules to formalise modeling operations and make their
static analysis possible. \maxime{À reformuler, douteux} Such rules can be
syntactically analysed to gather the events information before any rule
application. Thus, the time cost of the analysis is independent from the model
complexity. In some cases, a split or a merging can be detected and a
verification must done on the model to assert these events happened. However,
because of the way graph transformation rules work, any verification is
restricted to the modified cells as they are known during the application.
\maxime{paragraphe ré-écrit}

The analysis of an operation code is a particularly complexe task, even out of
reach. In order to circumvent this obstacle we propose to formalise geometric
models as generalized maps \cite{lienhardt1991topological} and their operations
with Jerboa graph transformation rules \cite{belhaouari2014jerboa}. The rules
syntactic analysis allow to predicate most of the events independently from the
modified object and its complexity. Thus, such an analysis can be computed once
before run-time.
